# Mosaic Editorial Knowledge — Seed Guide

This document contains foundational editorial principles derived from studying
top documentary-style YouTube channels. It bootstraps the Knowledge Base before
video analysis runs. Insights here are starting points — video analysis will
enrich and override them with observed evidence.

## Hook Principles

### Curiosity Gap Hook
Open with an unanswered question or paradox. The viewer's brain cannot rest until 
it is resolved. The hook works because it creates cognitive dissonance that demands 
closure — the viewer must keep watching to resolve the tension.
Application: state the paradox or question clearly within the first 15 seconds.
Never answer it until at least the 60% mark of the video.

### Statistic Shock Hook
Open with a number so large or so counterintuitive that it reframes the viewer's 
understanding immediately. Works because numbers feel like objective truth — they 
override the viewer's prior assumptions before they can resist.
Application: the statistic must contradict common belief. A statistic that confirms 
what people already think has no hook power.

### Second-Person Implication Hook
Address the viewer directly: "You have probably never thought about X, but..."
Works because it makes the video personally relevant before the viewer has decided 
whether to care. Bypasses the "this doesn't apply to me" filter.

## Story Arc Principles

### The Hidden Truth Arc
Structure: surface reality → complication → hidden mechanism → reframe
This arc works for incentives, economics, and systems content because it delivers 
the satisfaction of having your worldview corrected. The viewer feels smarter at 
the end than at the beginning.

### The Trend Arc
Structure: signal detection → stakes escalation → implication → future scenario
Works for geopolitical and technology content. The hook is "you haven't noticed 
this yet" — the payoff is "now you can't un-notice it."

## Visual Selection Principles

### Close-Up for Emotional Containment
A close-up of a single human face during an emotionally charged moment forces the 
viewer into intimacy. Wide shots diffuse emotion by distributing it across many 
subjects. Use close-ups at gut-punch moments to prevent emotional distance.

### Wide Shot for Revelation
After sustained tension, a wide establishing shot creates psychological release.
The viewer's visual field expands — they breathe again. Use at the moment of 
reframe or resolution, never before tension has built.

### Visual Contradiction for Irony
Pair visuals that contradict the narration's surface claim to create intellectual 
discomfort. The dissonance makes the point more memorable than the words alone.

## Pacing Principles

### Silence at the Gut-Punch
After delivering a shocking statistic or revelation, drop the music and narration 
for 1-1.5 seconds. This silence forces the viewer to sit with the information.
Breaking the silence too quickly robs the moment of its weight.

### Cut Rhythm as Tension Signal
Increasing cut frequency signals urgency and tension to the viewer's nervous system 
even before they consciously process the content. Slow cuts = contemplation. 
Fast cuts = momentum. Use this deliberately at each story phase transition.

### Music as Emotional Architecture
Music should shift at major story arc transitions, not at scene changes.
A music shift signals to the viewer subconsciously that the story has entered a 
new phase. Misaligned music shifts (at scene changes, not arc transitions) train 
the viewer to ignore music — wasting its emotional leverage entirely.

## Narration Style Principles

### Short Sentence at Impact
The most important sentences should be the shortest. A short sentence after a run 
of longer ones lands like a punch. "Seven hundred billion dollars. Gone."

### Rhetorical Question as Retention Tool
Asking the viewer a question mid-video re-engages attention that has drifted.
The question must be unanswerable without watching further.

### Rhythm Variation Prevents Habituation
Alternating sentence length (short-long-short-long) prevents the viewer's brain 
from habituating to the narrator's rhythm. Monotone rhythm produces passive 
listening. Varied rhythm demands active processing.
